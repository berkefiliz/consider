Showers in fact hide us from everything else

Trickery with human language is as old as humans themselves. Every time I think of modern clickbaits, I get reminded of Greenlight and Iceland, which is no more than a marketing scheme with a great backstory. If an adventurous 21st century sailor who does not believe in satellite imagery and photos would be greeted with immeasurable disappointment upon coming to close contact of the so-called green land.

This is why I have trust issues. - 9gag

Now, sitting by my computer and proudly criticizing outside world from the comfort of my chair, I feel the same kind of disappointment from time to time.It has not been long since I have clicked on an interesting article title to find out that the contents explain the complete opposite of what the title promised. Sadly, grabbing myself from the screens do not solve the issue. Our language itself is poisoned with a disease called false promises. In this post, I would like to elaborate on the nitpicky details of the English language that bother me on a daily basis.

We take suffixes for granted. If I would like to print 24 copies of my favourite meme, then I need to take a quick trip to my printer. If I realize that the room is a bit cold on the way there, then I will need to start the heater. Then I would probably play some rock on my music player. If Spotify blesses me with a nice album discovery, I show that album to a friend by using a showe-- wait no!

This is where the frustration begins to unfold. The language is built on simple premises yet an unfortunate string of characters create a visual trap for the simple-minded people like me. A systematic lie misguides me to believe that a shower is a tool that is used for displays. On a good day I would call this a false advertisement, but labeling the issue will not mend my crushed expectations. Now I have to sit down and take the pain of this realization to my grave, silently crying on the cold and lonely corners of the room.

I wish the name is at least partially correct too. A shower, for the most part, is located in the most hidden corner of the house. Sometimes it is behind a lock and sometimes it is covered with a curtain or a blurry glass. Its intended use is the complete opposite of what the name suggests. And we support this naming convention by not speaking up. What's next? Are we renaming speakers silencers? Or freezers burners? Where does it stop?

Maybe I need to take my time and put down the pitchforks before I declare war against whoever is maintaining the English language. This is no more than mere coincidence as the pattern of -er is quite common at the end of words. The genuine problem starts when a similar phenomenon occurs intentionally or as a result of the evolution of language.
